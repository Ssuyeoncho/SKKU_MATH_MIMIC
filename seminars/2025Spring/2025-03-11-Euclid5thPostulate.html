Even if you have not studied many advanced theories yet, there is plenty to explore on your own, and the more mathematics you study, the more fascinating questions this topic opens up. This seminar is especially intended for first-year students. You have probably heard many times that university mathematics is very different from high-school mathematics. Although we can only see a small part of it here, I hope this session gives you a sense of how university-level mathematics develops. I will also raise several questions during the talk, and I hope you continue thinking about them afterward as a form of intellectual play. This theme clearly shows how mathematicians have reflected on geometry since antiquity and how those reflections led to major theoretical developments. In the seminar, I will walk through that story of development. As noted above, the session is designed so that anyone can participate comfortably and think along. I remember briefly reading in high school that the sum of a triangle’s three interior angles might not always be 180 degrees. At the time, drawing a triangle on a sphere made it seem obvious, so I did not think much more about it. However, after returning from military service and taking a course called “General Geometry,” my perspective changed significantly. I came to feel how deeply and beautifully mathematicians had built the field of geometry through long, careful thought over many centuries. I wanted to share that joy through this seminar, so I chose this topic again even though speaker Jaehoon Yoo had presented the same theme at a MIMIC seminar two years ago. While preparing this seminar, I reopened textbooks and searched for references again. Some points I had previously passed over in “General Geometry” began to feel less rigorous when I revisited them, so I looked into them more carefully. Since clear explanations were not always easy to find online, I ended up taking paper and pen and drawing the figures one by one myself. Re-proving and understanding parts that look simple but are not trivial took time, but it was genuinely enjoyable.
