The Jacobi-Proximal Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (Jacobi-Proximal ADMM) is an optimization algorithm that allows multiple block variables to be updated in parallel. This makes it particularly useful for large-scale problems or problems with high computational complexity. However, a key limitation is that there has been relatively little theoretical analysis regarding its linear convergence—a crucial criterion for evaluating how quickly and reliably an algorithm produces results in practice. This gap motivated my research, and I chose this topic to share my findings with you. In addition, this seminar aims to go beyond simply using an algorithm. It will provide an opportunity to understand why the algorithm works well and how its behavior can be explained mathematically. During the seminar, I will analyze the convergence rate of the Jacobi-Proximal ADMM. This method was first introduced by Deng et al. and is applied to block-structured optimization problems with linear constraints. We will show that when the objective function is strongly convex and smooth, the algorithm achieves linear convergence. Through this seminar, participants will be able to follow the proof of linear convergence for the Jacobi-Proximal ADMM. Since establishing linear convergence provides important insight into the speed and stability of the algorithm, this analysis helps us better understand how the method can be applied in practice. As this is my first optimization paper published as a preprint on arXiv, I would like to take this opportunity to introduce it.
